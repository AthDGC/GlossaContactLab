Invited talks

Two invited lectures from the University of Bucharest.

Adina Dragomirescu

University of Bucharest

“The Syntax of Istro-Romanian: Between Romance and Slavonic”

This talk examines the syntax of Istro-Romanian, an endangered Romance variety spoken in Istria, within the context of intense contact with the Čakavian dialect of Croatian. Its structural grammar represents a unique intersection between archaic Romance inheritance and Slavonic syntactic reinforcement. Features like interpolation and auxiliary inversion reveal a relaxed V2 grammar where the verb moves to the C-domain. While contact with Croatian did not completely dictate these structures, it reinforced overlapping syntactic options that disappear in standard Romance languages.

Alexandru Nicolae

University of Bucharest

“The Syntax of Negation in Romanian from a Comparative Perspective”

This talk offers an overview of the negation system of Romanian, arguing that negation provides a reliable diagnostic for key aspects of clause structure. Romanian is of special interest because it displays strict negative concord, preserves morphosyntactic distinctions among negative markers (clitic nu vs affixal ne-), and occupies a hybrid position at the intersection of Romance inheritance and Balkan areal convergence. The analysis combines synchronic and diachronic perspectives and situates Romanian within a comparative context, with emphasis on the Balkan Sprachbund.

Young researchers

MA seminar contributions from doctoral, postdoctoral, and other early-stage researchers.

Maria Episkopou

“Beowulf, its translations and the diachronic importance of storytelling”

The presentation revolves around the language used in the epic of Beowulf and what it reveals about the era of both the original oral form and the manuscript. It also examines the role of kennings and other special forms, asking how translators have diachronically dealt with such elements and what their choices may show.

Georgia Stavrianopoulou

“From Abject to Subject: The Diachrony of Monstrous Frames in Fairytale Retellings”

Drawing on Frame Theory and fairytale studies, this presentation explores how contemporary retellings reframe monstrous figures as emotional beings and romantically desirable subjects.

Anastasia Tsiropina

“The diachrony of dative Object-Experiencer psych-verb constructions”

The study traces the development of dative object-experiencer psych-verbs in Greek diachrony using historical retranslations of Ancient Greek drama and compares the Greek findings with Modern English retranslations.

Eleni Plakoutsi

“Byzantine high and low registers: challenges for Early Medieval Greek studies”

The talk discusses the low prestige of Byzantine literature, diglossia, and the lack of reliable editions, and the problems these issues create for diachronic study.

Sophia Chionidi

“Computational Approaches to the Romance Influence on English Diachronic Morpho-Syntax: A Case of Historical Written Contact”

This talk examines written language contact in medieval England and discusses computational tools such as Python for extracting, cleaning, and visualizing diachronic linguistic data.

Vassilis Symeonidis

“Dynamic Complexity in Grammaticalization: A Corpus-Based Model of English Semi-Modals”

The presentation explores how grammatical complexity changes over time during grammaticalization and introduces a corpus-based computational model of dynamic complexity.

Evangelos Argyropoulos Theodoropoulos

"From Raw Text to Structured Linguistic Data"

One of the most valuable aspects of this experience was the interdisciplinary collaboration between linguistic analysis and computational implementation. The part of the workflow involved cooperation with the lab's IT and technical side, specifically through collaboration with Theodoros Michalareas.

The workflow constantly moved between linguistic logic and computational structure. Linguistic decisions often affected technical implementation, while technical constraints frequently influenced annotation and formatting decisions. In practice, this collaboration involved discussing workflow organization, adapting notebook pipelines, testing XML outputs, debugging malformed structures, and gradually improving automation processes.
